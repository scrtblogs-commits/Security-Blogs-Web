# SecurityBlogs — Tone of Voice

## Voice Summary

**Expert. Direct. Credible. Human.**

SecurityBlogs writes like a trusted senior consultant in the security industry — someone who knows the space deeply, respects the reader's intelligence, and gives them exactly what they need without fluff or filler.

---

## Four Core Voice Attributes

### 1. Expert
SecurityBlogs is the authority on security marketing. The writing demonstrates this through specificity, accurate industry terminology, and well-supported claims — not through jargon or gatekeeping.

**Sounds like:** "CCTV installers who rank in Google Maps local pack for commercial security in their service area generate 3–5× more inbound enquiries than those relying on word-of-mouth alone."

**Does not sound like:** "SEO is really important for businesses in today's digital landscape."

---

### 2. Direct
Get to the point. Security business owners are time-poor. They want to know what to do, why it matters, and what results to expect — in that order. No long preambles, no padding, no "great question" openers.

**Sounds like:** "Your website has three problems holding back your Google rankings: no schema markup, no location pages, and a site speed score below 50."

**Does not sound like:** "There are many different factors that can potentially impact your website's performance in search engines, and it can sometimes be difficult to know where to start..."

---

### 3. Credible
Every claim is backed by evidence. Stats, case studies, and specific examples build credibility. Opinion is clearly labelled as opinion. Industry claims reference real data.

**Sounds like:** "Clients on our Security SEO programme see an average of +180% organic traffic growth within 12 months — based on 50+ security brand engagements."

**Does not sound like:** "We've helped hundreds of businesses achieve incredible results with our proven methods."

---

### 4. Human
Despite the technical subject matter, SecurityBlogs writes for people — not search engines or robots. The writing has warmth, uses natural language, and acknowledges the real-world challenges security business owners face.

**Sounds like:** "If you've been told that SEO 'takes 6–12 months to work' without any further explanation, you were given a half-truth."

**Does not sound like:** "SecurityBlogs leverages cutting-edge SEO methodologies to synergize digital visibility and maximise ROI across all stakeholder touchpoints."

---

## Writing Rules

### Do
- Write in Australian English (not American English)
- Use active voice
- Write short sentences and short paragraphs (2–3 sentences max per paragraph in blog content)
- Use specific numbers and statistics
- Use headers and subheadings to create scannable structure
- Address the reader directly as "you"
- Name the industry and client type explicitly ("security installers," "CCTV companies")
- Explain technical concepts in plain language before using the acronym
- Use real examples from the security industry

### Don't
- Use corporate buzzwords ("synergy," "leverage," "holistic approach," "best-in-class")
- Use passive voice unless unavoidable
- Open with "In today's digital world..." or any variant
- Pad content with vague statements that don't add value
- Use American spelling (optimize → optimise, analyze → analyse, color → colour)
- Make claims without evidence
- Overuse exclamation marks
- Write AI-sounding filler sentences ("It's worth noting that..." / "It's important to remember...")

## Tone by Content Type

| Content Type | Tone Adjustment |
|---|---|
| **Blog posts** | Educational, confident, occasionally conversational |
| **Service pages** | Confident, results-focused, direct call to action |
| **Case studies** | Evidence-heavy, factual, client-outcome-led |
| **FAQs** | Plain English, concise, no-nonsense |
| **Social media** | Slightly more informal, punchy, single-idea per post |
| **Email** | Personal, direct, specific to the recipient's situation |
| **Technical SEO docs** | Precise, step-by-step, no ambiguity |

## Australian English Style Guide

| American | Australian |
|---|---|
| optimize | optimise |
| analyze | analyse |
| recognize | recognise |
| color | colour |
| behavior | behaviour |
| center | centre |
| license (verb) | licence (noun), license (verb) — context-dependent |
| program | programme (for planned activities); program (software) |
| gray | grey |

Use Australian currency (AUD), Australian date format (DD/MM/YYYY), and reference Australian regulations, cities, and industry bodies where relevant.

## Common Security Industry Terms to Use Correctly

- CCTV (not "security cameras" as the generic term in B2B context)
- Access control (not "door locks" or "card readers" — too imprecise)
- Intrusion detection (not "burglar alarms" in professional contexts)
- Integrator (a company that installs and integrates multiple security systems)
- Installer (a company that installs individual security products)
- End user (the business or property owner buying security systems — not the consumer)
- Specifier (architect, consultant, or engineer who specifies security systems)
- Monitoring station / alarm receiving centre (ARC)
- IP cameras / network cameras (vs. analogue)
- VMS (Video Management Software)
- PAC (Physical Access Control)
- Cybersecurity vs. physical security — always distinguish clearly

## Example: Before and After

**Before (generic, weak):**
"SecurityBlogs offers a wide range of SEO services that can help your business grow online. Our team of experts will work with you to develop a customised strategy that meets your unique needs and goals."

**After (SecurityBlogs voice):**
"SecurityBlogs specialises in SEO for security companies — CCTV installers, access control integrators, and security SaaS vendors. We don't serve retail or hospitality. We serve security, which means your strategy is built on keyword data, case studies, and competitive intelligence drawn exclusively from your industry."
