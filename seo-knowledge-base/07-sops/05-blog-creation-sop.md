# SOP 05 — Blog Creation

**Organisation:** SecurityBlogs Australia
**Department:** SEO / Digital Marketing
**Document Version:** 1.0
**Last Reviewed:** June 2026
**Review Frequency:** Quarterly

---

## Purpose

To provide a repeatable, quality-controlled process for creating SEO-optimised blog posts for SecurityBlogs Australia from brief review through to post-publish tasks. Every blog post produced must meet SEO standards, editorial quality standards, and Australian English requirements before and after publication.

---

## Scope

Applies to all blog posts published on SecurityBlogs Australia. Relevant to writers, SEO specialists, editors, and the content team lead. This SOP covers the full production workflow: brief → outline → draft → SEO pass → publish → post-publish.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1 — Brief Review

1. Writer receives the Content Brief (created via SOP 04 — Content Planning).
2. Before beginning any research or writing, the writer must review the brief in full and confirm:
   - Primary keyword is understood
   - Target word count is noted
   - Suggested outline has been reviewed
   - Tone and style requirements are clear (Australian English, professional-accessible)
   - Internal link requirements are noted
   - CTA is clear
3. If anything in the brief is unclear, the writer must raise questions with the content team lead **within 24 hours** of receiving the brief.
4. Writer confirms the brief is accepted by marking the task status as "In Progress" in the project management tool.

### Phase 2 — SERP and Research Analysis

5. Search the primary keyword in Google (incognito, location set to Australia where possible).
6. Review the top 5 ranking articles. Note:
   - Common headings and subheadings used
   - Content angles and unique value propositions
   - Approximate depth of coverage
   - What is missing or could be improved
7. Do not replicate existing content. Identify an angle or additional depth that will differentiate the SecurityBlogs article.
8. Collect the following research materials:
   - **Statistics:** Recent Australian or international security industry data, survey results, government reports (e.g., ACSC reports, ABS crime statistics)
   - **Expert quotes:** Quotes from ASIAL, security professionals, or reputable industry sources
   - **Industry data:** Product specs, regulatory information, standards references (e.g., AS 2201, NCC)
   - **Reference URLs:** 3–5 authoritative external sources to cite and link to
9. Save all research notes in the Google Doc below the outline.

### Phase 3 — Outline Creation (H2/H3 Structure)

10. Create the article outline in the Google Doc. The outline must include:
    - **H1:** The article title (incorporating the primary keyword, under 60 characters where possible)
    - **Introduction section** (no heading required)
    - **H2 headings:** 4–8 major sections covering the topic
    - **H3 subheadings** under H2s where required for further structure
    - **FAQ section** (if the SERP shows People Also Ask or if the brief specifies FAQPage schema)
    - **Conclusion section**
11. Ensure H2 headings incorporate secondary keywords and related terms from the brief naturally.
12. Share the outline with the content team lead for approval **before writing the first draft**, especially for pillar pages and long-form guides (1,500+ words).

### Phase 4 — First Draft

13. Write the first draft in the Google Doc following the approved outline.
14. Aim for the target word count (±10%).
15. Writing guidelines:
    - **First 100 words:** Include the primary keyword naturally in the opening paragraph
    - **Tone:** Professional, authoritative, and accessible. Written for security industry practitioners
    - **Australian English:** Use Australian spelling throughout (e.g., "organisation", "licence", "customise", "behaviour", "labour")
    - **Active voice:** Preferred throughout; passive voice sparingly
    - **Paragraphs:** Keep to 3–4 sentences maximum for readability
    - **Statistics:** Always cite the source inline and link to it
    - **No fluff:** Every paragraph must serve the reader's intent
16. Write the conclusion last. Include the primary CTA from the brief.
17. Mark the draft as "First Draft Complete" in the Google Doc and notify the content team lead.

### Phase 5 — SEO Optimisation Pass

18. Before submitting for editorial review, the writer (or SEO specialist) conducts an SEO pass:

**Keyword Placement:**
- [ ] Primary keyword in the H1 title
- [ ] Primary keyword within the first 100 words of the introduction
- [ ] Primary keyword in at least one H2 heading (or naturally in H3 if H2 use would be forced)
- [ ] Primary keyword in the meta title (see step 22)
- [ ] Primary keyword in the meta description (see step 22)
- [ ] Primary keyword in the URL slug
- [ ] Primary keyword used 2–4 more times throughout the body (not stuffed)
- [ ] Secondary keywords incorporated naturally in H2s and body paragraphs
- [ ] LSI (latent semantic indexing) terms and related phrases used throughout (refer to brief or use Surfer SEO content score for guidance)

**Heading Structure:**
- [ ] Only one H1 per page
- [ ] H2s are used for major sections
- [ ] H3s are used for subsections under H2s (no skipping heading levels)
- [ ] Headings are descriptive and readable (not keyword-stuffed)

**Content Quality:**
- [ ] Word count meets target (±10%)
- [ ] All statistics cited with source links
- [ ] No duplicate content present (original writing throughout)

### Phase 6 — Meta Data

19. Write the **meta title:**
    - 50–60 characters (including spaces)
    - Primary keyword near the beginning
    - Includes brand or location modifier where relevant (e.g., "| SecurityBlogs Australia")
    - Compelling and click-worthy
20. Write the **meta description:**
    - 140–160 characters
    - Includes primary keyword
    - Describes what the reader will gain from the article
    - Includes a subtle CTA (e.g., "Read our guide", "Find out more")
21. Write the **URL slug:**
    - Lowercase, hyphens only (no underscores, no spaces)
    - Includes primary keyword
    - Short and descriptive (3–5 words ideally)
    - No stop words (the, a, and, of, in) unless essential for meaning

### Phase 7 — Internal Links Insertion

22. Insert a minimum of **3 internal links** per post:
    - Link to the pillar page of the topic cluster this post belongs to
    - Link to 2–4 relevant cluster posts or supporting pages
    - Use natural, descriptive anchor text — avoid generic "click here" anchors
    - Ensure anchor text varies (do not use the exact same anchor text for the same destination URL across multiple posts)
23. Identify existing pages on the site that should link TO this new post and list them in the Google Doc for the post-publish phase.

### Phase 8 — Image Sourcing and Alt Text

24. Source a **featured image** (hero image for the top of the post):
    - Use licensed stock photography (Getty, Shutterstock, Unsplash) or original photography
    - Relevance to the security topic is required
    - Minimum resolution: 1200 x 628px for social sharing compatibility
    - File format: JPEG or WebP; compress to under 150KB without quality loss
25. Source any **in-post images, diagrams, or infographics** referenced in the outline.
26. For every image, write descriptive **alt text:**
    - Describes what is in the image accurately
    - Incorporates the primary or secondary keyword where it is genuinely relevant and natural
    - Maximum 125 characters
    - Does not start with "Image of" or "Photo of"
27. Name all image files descriptively before uploading (e.g., `access-control-system-australia.jpg` not `IMG_00234.jpg`).

### Phase 9 — Schema Markup

28. Confirm the appropriate schema markup is applied in WordPress (via Yoast SEO or Rank Math):
    - **Article schema:** Required on all blog posts (author, publish date, headline, image)
    - **FAQPage schema:** Required if an FAQ section is present (mark up each Q&A pair)
    - **BreadcrumbList schema:** Should be site-wide via plugin
29. If the post includes a How-To section, apply **HowTo schema** markup.
30. After publishing, validate schema using the **Google Rich Results Test**.

### Phase 10 — Editorial Proofread

31. The editor (or content team lead) reviews the final draft for:
    - **Grammar and spelling:** Australian English throughout (run Grammarly set to Australian English as a secondary check; do not rely on Grammarly alone)
    - **Factual accuracy:** All statistics, legal references, and product information verified
    - **Tone and style consistency:** Matches the SecurityBlogs voice guide
    - **Flow and readability:** Paragraphs connect logically; no abrupt transitions
    - **Heading structure:** Confirmed correct (one H1, logical H2/H3 hierarchy)
    - **Internal links:** All present and functional
    - **CTA:** Present and correct
32. All edits are made using Google Docs **Suggesting** mode so the writer can review changes.
33. Writer reviews and accepts/rejects suggestions. Final version approved by content team lead.

### Phase 11 — Publish Checklist

Before clicking Publish in WordPress, confirm the following:

- [ ] Title (H1) set correctly in WordPress
- [ ] URL slug is correct (not auto-generated from a keyword-stuffed title)
- [ ] Meta title entered in Yoast/Rank Math (do not leave blank)
- [ ] Meta description entered
- [ ] Correct category and tags assigned
- [ ] Featured image uploaded and set
- [ ] All images have alt text
- [ ] Article schema confirmed active
- [ ] FAQPage schema applied (if FAQ section present)
- [ ] Internal links functional (click-test each one in the draft preview)
- [ ] All external links open in a new tab and are not broken
- [ ] Mobile preview checked in WordPress (preview on mobile viewport)
- [ ] Publish date and author set correctly
- [ ] Post is set to the correct visibility (Public, not Draft or Private)

### Phase 12 — Post-Publish Tasks

Immediately after publishing:

34. **Google Search Console — URL Inspection:**
    - Go to GSC > URL Inspection
    - Enter the new post URL
    - Click **Request Indexing**
    - Record the date of indexing request in the editorial calendar

35. **Internal Link Updates on Related Pages:**
    - Return to the list of existing pages identified in Phase 7 (Step 23) that should link to this new post
    - Edit each of those pages to add an internal link to the new post with appropriate anchor text
    - Aim to complete this within 24 hours of publishing

36. **Social Sharing:**
    - Share the post on SecurityBlogs' LinkedIn page (primary channel for security industry B2B)
    - Share to Facebook page
    - Share to any relevant industry LinkedIn groups
    - Notify any experts quoted in the article so they can share or comment
    - Schedule follow-up shares at 7 days and 30 days using the social scheduling tool

37. **Email Newsletter (if applicable):**
    - If the post is included in the current newsletter cycle, provide the URL and excerpt to the newsletter coordinator

38. **Track in Editorial Calendar:**
    - Update the editorial calendar task status to "Published"
    - Record the live URL
    - Set a **90-day review reminder** to check the post's ranking progress in GSC

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Writing before reviewing the SERP** — without knowing what already ranks, it is impossible to differentiate or improve upon existing content.
- **Keyword stuffing** — the primary keyword should appear naturally, not be forced into every sentence. Google's algorithms detect unnatural keyword density.
- **Ignoring the post-publish tasks** — publishing is not the end of the workflow. Internal link updates and GSC indexing requests are critical for rapid ranking progress.
- **Using American English** — SecurityBlogs Australia must use Australian English throughout. Set all writing and grammar tools to Australian English.
- **Skipping alt text** — image alt text is both an accessibility requirement and an SEO signal. No image should be published without it.
- **Uploading uncompressed images** — large image files are one of the most common causes of slow page load times. Always compress before uploading.
- **Publishing without a meta description** — leaving the meta description blank allows Google to auto-generate one, which is often poorly optimised.
