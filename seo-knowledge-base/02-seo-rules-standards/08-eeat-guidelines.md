## Overview

E-E-A-T stands for **Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness**. It is the framework Google's quality raters use to evaluate content quality, and it directly influences how Google's algorithms assess and rank pages — particularly in YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) adjacent topics, which include business decision-making, professional services, and industry guidance.

SecurityBlogs is not a medical or financial site in the traditional YMYL sense, but security industry content carries meaningful real-world stakes: security licencing, business compliance, staff vetting, and physical safety decisions. Google applies elevated quality scrutiny to content in this space.

Strong E-E-A-T is not achieved by a single tactic — it is the cumulative result of many deliberate signals embedded in content, site structure, author profiles, and third-party references. This document outlines what each dimension of E-E-A-T means for SecurityBlogs and provides actionable requirements for every editorial and SEO decision.

## 1. Experience (The First E)

Experience refers to **first-hand, real-world experience** with the subject matter. Google distinguishes between someone who has genuinely engaged with a topic (visited a security industry conference, worked in the sector, interviewed industry professionals) versus someone who has only researched it at arm's length.

Experience is the newest addition to the E-E-A-T framework (added 2022) and reflects Google's push against generic, AI-generated, or superficial content that lacks genuine depth.

### 1.1 How SecurityBlogs Demonstrates Experience

**Real industry coverage:**
- Articles must reference real events, real companies, and real industry developments in Australia
- Content about the security industry should be grounded in what is actually happening — regulatory changes, ASIAL updates, licensing reforms, industry trends
- Blog posts should cite real examples (with permission or public source), case studies, or direct quotes from industry professionals

**Named authors with genuine backgrounds:**
- Every article must be attributed to a named author (see Section 5 — Author Requirements)
- Author bios must describe actual relevant experience — not vague credentials
- Where an author has direct security industry experience (e.g., former security officer, licencing consultant, industry journalist), this must be stated explicitly

**Original research and observations:**
- Commission or conduct original research where possible (surveys of Australian security businesses, analysis of search trend data, review of industry licensing statistics)
- Reference first-party observations: "We reviewed 50 security company websites in Australia and found…"

**On-site experience signals:**
- Product/service reviews must be based on genuine use or genuine client outcomes — not rephrased manufacturer descriptions
- Where SecurityBlogs writes about a service or tool, note whether the team has used it

### 1.2 Experience Red Flags (Avoid)

- Generic articles that could apply to any industry without any real security-specific detail
- Recycled statistics not verified against Australian sources
- "Tips" articles that contain no real-world examples or security industry context
- Content clearly written without any genuine industry knowledge

## 2. Expertise

Expertise refers to **subject matter knowledge** — the depth of understanding demonstrated in the content itself. A page can signal expertise through accuracy, depth, correct use of technical terminology, and clear understanding of how concepts interact.

### 2.1 How SecurityBlogs Demonstrates Expertise

**Technical accuracy:**
- All security industry facts must be verified against authoritative Australian sources (ASIAL, state licensing bodies, government publications)
- SEO, AEO, GEO, and digital marketing advice must reflect current best practices — not outdated tactics
- Writers covering security licensing must understand the legislative framework in each state
- Writers covering digital marketing must understand the difference between SEO, AEO, GEO, GBP optimisation, and Google Ads — and write accordingly

**Use of correct industry terminology:**
- Use Australian security industry terminology correctly (see `07-australian-english-rules.md` Section 7)
- Demonstrate familiarity with the licensing structure (Class 1/2/3, Master Licence, crowd controller licence, etc.)
- Use correct digital marketing terminology without oversimplification

**Depth of coverage:**
- Service pages must explain not just what SecurityBlogs offers but why it matters, how it works, and what results it achieves
- Blog posts must go beyond surface-level overviews — include specific, actionable detail that demonstrates genuine understanding
- Comparison content must show genuine analysis, not just listing features side by side

**Author credentials:**
- Author bios must include relevant expertise signals: education (if applicable), relevant experience, specific areas of expertise, publications or industry contributions
- Link author bios to their published work on SecurityBlogs
- Where an author is a recognised industry figure, link to external verification of that recognition

**Mentioning industry certifications and associations:**
- Reference relevant Australian certifications where applicable to the topic (e.g., CPP — Certified Protection Professional, ASIS membership, ASIAL membership)
- Note when SecurityBlogs personnel or contributors hold relevant certifications
- Do not claim certifications that are not held

### 2.2 Expertise Red Flags (Avoid)

- Shallow content that explains concepts only at the most basic level
- Errors in security industry terminology or licensing facts
- SEO advice that references outdated tactics (e.g., keyword density targets, exact-match domain obsession)
- Author bios that are vague or empty of genuine credentials

## 3. Authoritativeness

Authoritativeness is largely an **off-page and site-level signal** — it reflects how recognised SecurityBlogs is as a credible source within the security industry and digital marketing communities. Authority is earned through third-party acknowledgement: backlinks, mentions, citations, and coverage.

### 3.1 How SecurityBlogs Builds Authority

**Backlink profile:**
- Earn links from reputable Australian security industry sites: ASIAL, state government agencies, security company websites, industry news outlets
- Earn links from reputable Australian digital marketing and SEO sites
- Prioritise quality over quantity — one link from a relevant, trusted Australian domain outweighs 50 links from irrelevant or low-quality sites
- Avoid link schemes, reciprocal link farms, and paid link arrangements that violate Google's guidelines

**Brand mentions:**
- Secure brand mentions in Australian security industry media (print and digital)
- Contribute quotes, data, and expert commentary to other publications (with attribution to SecurityBlogs)
- Guest contributions by SecurityBlogs team members to industry associations and publications

**Domain Authority (DA) and Domain Rating (DR):**
- Monitor DA (Moz) and DR (Ahrefs) as directional indicators — not as absolute targets
- Track referring domains quarterly and identify link-building opportunities
- Target: grow to DR 40+ from the Australian security industry vertical over 24 months

**Wikipedia and authoritative directory listings:**
- Ensure SecurityBlogs is listed accurately on relevant Australian business directories (True Local, Yellow Pages, ASIAL directory)
- Create and maintain a Google Business Profile
- Where appropriate, establish Wikipedia references (editorial guidelines apply — do not create self-promotional Wikipedia content)

**Social proof and community recognition:**
- Build an engaged audience on LinkedIn and industry forums
- Seek endorsements from recognisable Australian security industry figures
- Publish content that is shared and referenced by industry professionals

### 3.2 Authority Red Flags (Avoid)

- Buying links or participating in link exchange schemes
- Claiming authority that is not yet established ("Australia's number one…" without evidence)
- Publishing content only for SEO with no genuine value to the security industry community
- Ignoring third-party coverage opportunities

## 4. Trustworthiness

Trustworthiness is the foundation of E-E-A-T — Google's quality rater guidelines note that trust is the most important dimension. A trustworthy site is transparent about who it is, who creates its content, how that content is produced, and how it handles user data and commercial relationships.

### 4.1 Transparency and Disclosure

**Editorial disclosure labels:**
All content on SecurityBlogs must be clearly labelled by type:

| Label | When to Use |
|---|---|
| `Editorial` or `Article` | Independent editorial content with no commercial arrangement |
| `Sponsored Post` | Content for which payment or free access was received |
| `Guest Post` | Content written by an external contributor (who may or may not have paid for placement) |
| `Partner Content` | Content produced in collaboration with a commercial partner |
| `Advertisement` | Clearly identified paid advertising content |
| `Opinion` | Opinion pieces or editorials expressing a personal view |

**Rule:** Never publish commercially influenced content without a clear disclosure. This is both an ethical requirement and a Google guideline requirement (per Google's spam policies on disguised advertising).

**About page:**
- The About page must clearly describe what SecurityBlogs is, who owns and operates it, and what its mission is
- Include information about editorial standards and processes
- Update whenever significant changes occur to ownership or editorial structure

**Author bylines:**
- Every article must have a named author
- Author names must link to a complete author bio page
- Anonymous or "Staff Writer" bylines are not permitted for editorial content
- Where a contractor or external writer contributed, they must still be named

**Contact information:**
- SecurityBlogs must maintain an accessible, clearly labelled Contact page
- Include an email address or contact form
- Include a physical address (Melbourne, VIC) for organisational legitimacy
- Response to contact enquiries within 2 business days (trust signal for users)

### 4.2 Privacy and Data Handling

- Maintain an up-to-date Privacy Policy page compliant with the *Privacy Act 1988* (Cth) and the Australian Privacy Principles (APPs)
- Display a clear cookie consent notice compliant with Australian law
- Do not sell or share user data without disclosure
- Clearly label any email sign-up forms with what users are subscribing to

### 4.3 Accuracy and Corrections Policy

- SecurityBlogs commits to factual accuracy and must correct significant errors promptly
- When an article is corrected after publication, note the correction at the top of the article:  
  `Correction [Date]: An earlier version of this article stated [X]. This has been corrected to [Y].`
- Do not silently edit factual errors without acknowledgement
- Maintain an editorial corrections log internally

### 4.4 Avoiding Misinformation and Sensationalism

- Never publish unverified claims about security companies, individuals, or regulatory bodies
- Do not use sensationalist or alarmist language unsupported by facts
- Ensure all statistics are attributed to a named source
- Where data cannot be verified, clearly note that it is unverified or approximate

## 5. Author Requirements (Supporting All Four E-E-A-T Dimensions)

Named, credentialed authors are one of the strongest E-E-A-T signals available. Every article published on SecurityBlogs must meet these author requirements.

### 5.1 Author Bio Page Requirements

Each author bio page must include:

- Full name (not a pseudonym)
- Professional role or title
- 2–4 sentence biography explaining their experience and expertise in the security industry, digital marketing, or the specific subject matter they write about
- Photo (professional headshot preferred)
- LinkedIn profile link (required) and any other relevant professional profiles
- List of or link to articles published on SecurityBlogs
- Any relevant certifications, memberships, or qualifications

**Good author bio example:**
> *Jane Smith is a digital marketing strategist with seven years' experience working with Australian security companies. She has assisted over 30 security guard businesses improve their Google rankings and has contributed to ASIAL's online resources. Jane holds a Google Ads certification and a Certificate IV in Marketing and Communication.*

**Poor author bio example:**
> *Jane is a writer who covers digital marketing and security topics.*

### 5.2 External / Guest Authors

Guest authors published through the guest posting programme must also meet E-E-A-T standards:

- Must be identifiable, real individuals (no anonymous guest posts)
- Bio must describe genuine relevance to the security industry or the topic written about
- Guest post content undergoes editorial review before publication
- Disclosure label "Guest Post" must be applied
- If the guest post is a paid placement, the label "Sponsored Guest Post" must be used

## 6. Actionable E-E-A-T Checklist

Use this checklist for every piece of content published on SecurityBlogs.

### Experience
- [ ] Article references real Australian security industry events, companies, or developments
- [ ] Content is written by someone with genuine knowledge of the topic (verified by bio)
- [ ] Any claims of personal experience or first-hand knowledge are accurate
- [ ] Statistics and data are sourced from genuine Australian sources, not fabricated
- [ ] Generic content that could apply to any industry has been made security-specific

### Expertise
- [ ] Security industry terminology is used correctly and consistently
- [ ] All factual claims about licensing, regulations, or industry practices are verified
- [ ] Digital marketing advice reflects current, accurate best practices
- [ ] Author bio demonstrates genuine expertise relevant to the topic
- [ ] Content is sufficiently deep — not a surface-level overview of a complex topic

### Authoritativeness
- [ ] The article, guide, or page adds genuine value not found in existing content on the topic
- [ ] At least one external authority source is linked to (government site, ASIAL, peer-reviewed research)
- [ ] SecurityBlogs is credited correctly in any external references or quotes used
- [ ] Where relevant, SecurityBlogs has sought backlinks or mentions from third-party industry sites
- [ ] Content is comprehensive enough to be a reference for others in the industry

### Trustworthiness
- [ ] Content type is clearly labelled (Editorial, Sponsored, Guest Post, etc.)
- [ ] Author is named with a link to a complete bio page
- [ ] All statistics are attributed to a named source
- [ ] No unverified claims or sensationalist language
- [ ] Corrections policy applied if updating a previously published article
- [ ] Privacy Policy, Contact page, and About page are all current and accessible
- [ ] Any commercial relationships disclosed appropriately

## 7. Site-Wide E-E-A-T Signals

In addition to individual article quality, the following site-wide elements contribute to E-E-A-T and must be maintained:

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| About page | Accurate, current, describes mission and editorial process |
| Contact page | Live email or form; physical address listed |
| Privacy Policy | Compliant with Australian Privacy Principles; updated annually |
| Terms of Service | Current and accessible from footer |
| Editorial Standards page | Describes how content is created, fact-checked, and corrected |
| Author bio pages | All active authors have complete, current bio pages |
| SSL certificate | Site must be served entirely over HTTPS |
| Google Business Profile | Claimed, verified, and kept current |
| Social profiles | Active, consistent with site content and branding |
