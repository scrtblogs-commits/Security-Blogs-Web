# Indirect Competitors — General SEO Agencies, Freelancers & DIY Platforms

> **Scope:** Entities that are not security-specialist but compete for the same marketing budget from security industry prospects. Includes large Australian SEO agencies, freelance consultants, and DIY SEO/marketing platforms that a security business might choose instead of SecurityBlogs.

**Last Updated:** June 2026
**Review Cycle:** Quarterly

---

## Why Indirect Competitors Matter

Security industry prospects frequently evaluate SecurityBlogs against alternatives they already know:

1. **Large full-service SEO agencies** they have seen advertised heavily (King Kong, Online Marketing Gurus)
2. **Cheaper freelance alternatives** found via Upwork, LinkedIn, or referral
3. **DIY platforms** they already pay subscriptions for (Semrush, Ahrefs, HubSpot)
4. **Their web developer** who "does SEO on the side"
5. **In-house marketing staff** or a newly hired marketing coordinator

Understanding each category's pitch and weaknesses allows SecurityBlogs to pre-empt objections and reframe every comparison in terms of security-specific outcomes.

## Category B: Freelance SEO Consultants

### 6. Independent SEO Consultants (Upwork / Local Referral)
- **Platform:** Upwork, PeoplePerHour, LinkedIn, local referral networks
- **Pricing:** $50–$200/hour or $500–$2,500/month retainers
- **Typical Profile:** 1–2 person operation; generalist SEO practitioner with broad industry background
- **Strengths:**
  - Lower cost — most attractive to price-sensitive SME security businesses
  - Direct access to the consultant — no account manager layer
  - Flexible engagement terms — month-to-month with no lock-in
  - Personal relationships and responsiveness
- **Weaknesses vs SecurityBlogs:**
  - No security industry knowledge — cannot write authoritative security content or understand the buyer journey
  - No content creation capacity for technical security topics at scale
  - No owned media or link building assets in the security niche — links will come from generic, low-relevance sources
  - Single-person bottleneck — no capacity for scale, sick days create gaps, holidays stall projects
  - No AEO, GEO, or AI Visibility capability — these require platform-level knowledge and testing environments
  - No advertising inventory, newsletter, or industry relationships
  - No accountability — freelancers can disappear, pivot to other work, or simply underdeliver without consequence
- **SecurityBlogs Counter-Pitch:** "A freelancer can optimise your page titles and maybe write a few blog posts. SecurityBlogs can get your brand appearing in AI search results for 'best security company Melbourne,' place a feature article on our DA 68 publication, send your announcement to 24,000 security professionals, and build an organic visibility strategy for your entire security product line. That is a fundamentally different scale of capability."
- **Prospect Signal:** Prospects comparing SecurityBlogs to a freelancer are typically price-sensitive SMEs or early-stage security businesses. Qualify the budget carefully. If they cannot invest appropriately in marketing, a freelancer may be the right starting point — acknowledge this honestly and create a pathway to upgrade when they are ready.

### 7. Web Developers Offering SEO Add-Ons
- **Profile:** Local web studios, WordPress/WooCommerce developers who bundle "basic SEO" with web builds
- **Platform:** Referrals, web design project upsells, local business networks
- **Pricing:** Bundled with web builds ($0–$500 for "SEO setup") or $300–$800/month for ongoing
- **Typical Capability:** Installing Yoast SEO, adding a meta description, submitting a sitemap, ticking the "SEO setup" box on a project checklist
- **Strengths:**
  - Existing trusted relationship — they built the website, so the client already trusts them
  - Single-vendor simplicity — website and SEO from one provider
  - Low cost — often bundled into existing web maintenance contracts
  - Proximity — usually local, responsive, known face
- **Weaknesses vs SecurityBlogs:**
  - SEO as afterthought, not core competency — typically no keyword research, content strategy, or link building
  - Technical SEO often configured incorrectly — canonical errors, poor crawl budgets, missing schema markup
  - No security industry knowledge — cannot create industry-relevant content
  - No link building, no editorial network, no publication placement capability
  - No understanding of AEO, GEO, AI Visibility, or modern search behaviour
  - Lack of accountability — SEO is a side service with no SLA or performance metrics
- **SecurityBlogs Counter-Pitch / Tactic:** Education play — offer a free technical SEO audit that surfaces the gaps their web developer has created. Show specific missed opportunities: keyword positions lost, pages not indexed, schema errors, Core Web Vitals failures, missing structured data. Convert the audit into a proposal.
- **Prospect Signal:** "Our web developer looks after our SEO" is one of the most common objections in the security SME market. The audit-to-close strategy is the most effective counter.

## Category C: DIY SEO & Marketing Platforms

### 8. Semrush (DIY)
- **Type:** SaaS SEO platform
- **Website:** semrush.com
- **Pricing:** $129–$499+/month (individual plans); $5,000+/year for agency/enterprise
- **Typical User:** In-house marketing managers, business owners attempting self-managed SEO
- **Strengths:**
  - Powerful keyword and competitor research tools — genuinely useful for keyword discovery
  - Comprehensive site audit capability — surfaces technical SEO issues
  - Content marketing workflow and topic clustering features (Content Shake AI, SEO Writing Assistant)
  - Social media management, backlink analysis, PPC research — broad platform
  - Brand recognition — many security prospects already have subscriptions
  - Position Tracking and reporting dashboards suit in-house teams
- **Weaknesses vs SecurityBlogs:**
  - Tool only — provides data but no execution, no strategy development, no content creation
  - Requires significant SEO knowledge to interpret and act on data correctly — most security business owners do not have this skill set
  - No security industry context built in — keyword suggestions are generic, not security-buyer-specific
  - No link building execution, no guest posting, no AI Visibility capability
  - Time cost on the security business owner is significant — hours spent in Semrush are hours away from running the business
  - Content creation still requires internal resource or additional contractor expense
- **Objection:** "We already use Semrush — why would we also pay an agency?"
- **SecurityBlogs Response:** "Semrush tells you what to do — SecurityBlogs does it for you, with security industry expertise built in. The tool is only as good as the strategy and execution behind it. Many of our clients use Semrush; we use it too. But the gap between having the data and turning it into security industry rankings is exactly what we fill."
- **Prospect Signal:** A security business using Semrush but not seeing results is a warm prospect — they are already investing in SEO awareness, they understand the concept, and they have evidence that DIY is not working.

## Key Takeaway for Sales Conversations

Every indirect competitor — from King Kong to a $99/month Ahrefs subscription — shares the same structural limitation: **they cannot give a security business what SecurityBlogs has already built.**

A DA 68 security industry publication, 180,000 monthly readers, 24,000 newsletter subscribers, security-specific keyword expertise, and AEO/GEO/AI Visibility services are not services anyone can replicate quickly. They represent years of compounded effort in a niche that generalists cannot enter easily.

The sales task is to make prospects understand that they are not choosing between two similar services at different price points. They are choosing between a generic tool and a security industry ecosystem.
